A strong **build a hooper center build** should solve one clear problem: win possessions near the rim. That means setting screens, protecting the paint, finishing easy looks, and owning the glass. If you try to do everything at once, the build usually feels softer than it should.

Think of the center slot as your team’s anchor. Your job is not just scoring; it is controlling space. When the build is clean, every rebound, contest, and putback creates momentum. That is why a center build should start with role clarity before any attribute spending.

<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Attribute Priorities and Body Settings">

The safest way to build a center is to spend points in the order the position actually uses them. Start with the skills that decide possessions, then move into support stats. If a stat does not help you win rebounds, protect the rim, or score efficient points, it should usually wait.

That approach keeps the build stable. It also makes the player easier to pilot in early games, when you are still learning movement, spacing, and timing. For most users, the best center build is not the flashiest one; it is the one that gets consistent stops and second-chance points.

| Attribute Group | Priority | What to Maximize | Skip Early |
|---|---|---|---|
| Interior Defense | High | Rim contests, blocks | Low-impact perimeter stats |
| Rebounding | High | Box outs, boards | Fancy scoring upgrades |
| Strength | High | Screens, seals, contact wins | Unneeded speed premiums |
| Finishing | Medium | Efficient layups and dunks | Low-percentage shot creation |
| Playmaking | Medium | Simple outlets, short passes | Dribble-heavy creation |
| Shooting | Optional | Short-range safety | Deep-range luxury |

| Build Type | Height Direction | Weight Direction | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paint Anchor | Taller | Heavier | Protect the rim and finish inside |
| Balanced Center | Medium-tall | Middle ground | Handle switches and keep mobility |
| Mobile Big | Slightly shorter | Leaner | Hedge, recover, and run the floor |

<Callout type="warning" title="Do Not Overbuild One Stat">
A center with too much luxury offense can feel nice in practice, but it may lose value if rebounds and contests drop off.
</Callout>

When you compare body setups, ask one simple question: “What am I supposed to win?” If the answer is the paint, then size and strength matter more than extra dribbling. If the answer is versatility, then mobility deserves a bigger slice of the build. That choice should shape every point you spend.

<Callout type="tip" title="Practical Spending Order">
Use this order when you are unsure: defense first, rebounding second, strength third, finishing fourth, and only then optional shooting or passing.
</Callout>

<Section icon={Map} title="How to Build the Player Step by Step">

The cleanest center build comes from a simple decision path. Do not start with random sliders or favorite animations. Start with the role, then lock the physical profile, and only then fill the attribute gaps. That keeps the build coherent and makes later adjustments much easier.

Use the steps below as a repeatable setup method. It works whether you want a bruiser in the paint or a more mobile defensive big.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Choose the center identity">
    Decide whether your player will be a paint anchor, a board-crashing finisher, or a mobile help defender.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Lock the body profile">
    Pick a size that matches the role. Bigger builds win contact, while leaner builds recover faster on switches.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Spend on core possessions">
    Put your first points into interior defense, rebounding, and strength so the build affects every possession.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Add support tools">
    Fill in finishing, passing, or short-range shooting only after your defensive base already feels solid.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Step | What Success Looks Like | Common Mistake | Better Adjustment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Role choice | One clear job on the court | Mixing every playstyle | Focus on one primary identity |
| Body setup | Size matches movement needs | Choosing size for looks only | Tune the body to the role |
| Core spending | Immediate board and contest value | Overinvesting in scoring | Build win-condition stats first |
| Support tools | Useful extras, not distractions | Buying too many luxury upgrades | Add only what you will use |

<Callout type="warning" title="Early Build Mistake">
If the build feels slow and empty, the problem is often role confusion, not a lack of points. Re-center the build around one job.
</Callout>

A good checkpoint is this: after the first few games, you should already feel your presence in the paint. If you are not changing shot selection, boxing out, or ending possessions, the build needs more focus. Small corrections now save a lot of rebuilding later.

A center build becomes valuable through habits, not just numbers. The right defensive habit can make an average matchup easier, and the wrong habit can make a strong build look slow. Your goal is to play connected basketball: contest, rebound, secure the possession, then reset quickly.

Use your first sessions to learn what kind of big you really are. If you dominate inside, lean into that. If you move well, use your mobility to cover help situations and recover on kickouts. The build should reinforce your instincts, not fight them.

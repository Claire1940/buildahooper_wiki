<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Turn Footage Into a Build Plan">

Once you know what you are watching, convert that observation into a plan. The goal is not to memorize a creator’s exact numbers. The goal is to understand which choices make the build feel stable in real play.

<Callout type="warning" title="Avoid One-Clip Decisions">
Do not lock in a build after watching only one strong performance. Compare at least two clips with the same role before you commit.
</Callout>

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Pick one role">
    Decide whether you want to build around playmaking, scoring, two-way impact, or interior dominance.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Record the priorities">
    Write down the position, the main strengths, and the first attributes that get attention.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Compare one alternative">
    Check a second clip with the same position so you can see what changes and what stays consistent.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Test and revise">
    Try the build in short sessions, then adjust the weakest part before pushing deeper into progression.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| What You Notice | What to Do Next |
|---|---|
| Low shooting usage | Lower shooting investment and strengthen another core area |
| Frequent turnovers | Raise ball control or passing support |
| Easy interior scoring | Prioritize finishing or strength |
| Weak defensive stops | Add a defensive stat budget |
| Slow movement | Improve speed or reduce overcommitment to heavy builds |

This process keeps your build intentional. Instead of chasing trends, you are building around repeatable strengths. That is the difference between a fun run and a frustrating reset.

A good plan also leaves room for growth. If the footage shows a build that only works at peak investment, treat it as an advanced template, not a starter blueprint.

</Section>
